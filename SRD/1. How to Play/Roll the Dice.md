Ability Tests are quick and straightforward, with a custom dice system that not only tells you whether you succeed or fail, but also how well you succeed (or how gravely you fail!).

To resolve an action, we go through these steps:

###### 1. The player states their goal and chooses their ability
Your goal is outcome you want your character to achieve when they overcome the obstacle at hand. Some example goals are “I want to get into the manor house” or “I want to see who comes and goes at the house.” In both cases, the obstacle is the guards. They are a challenging obstacle that may be dangerous or troublesome for the Players.

Usually the character’s goal is pretty obvious by their ability choice, but the group should ask questions to the player to clarify their goal if they don't understand how it could be achieved with the ability.

You can’t roll an ability unless your character performs that action in the fiction. i.e. If you want to roll your Command action, then you must be ordering someone around.

###### potency

The potency factor considers particular weaknesses, taking extra time or a bigger risk, or the influence of arcane powers. The electrical discharge of a lightning hook is potent against a ghost. The supernatural powers of a ghost are potent against a human. An infiltrator is more potent if all the lights are extinguished and they move about in the dark.

quality / tier

Quality represents the effectiveness of tools, weapons, or other resources, usually summarized by Tier. Fine items count as +1 bonus in quality, stacking with Tier.

Arlyn is picking the lock to a safehouse run by the Circle of Flame. Her crew is Tier I and she has fine lockpicks—so she’s effectively Tier II. The Circle are Tier III. Arlyn is outclassed in quality, so her effect will be limited on the lock.

scale

Scale represents the number of opponents, size of an area covered, scope of influence, etc. Larger scale can be an advantage or disadvantage depending on the situation. In battle, more people are better. When infiltrating, more people are a hindrance.

###### Beneficial Situation
You’re exploiting a golden opportunity. You’re set up for success, but the outcome isn't guaranteed. Some examples would be; surprising an enemy who is on guard, sneaking behind someone to attack from behind, getting someone a few drinks before charming them etc.

*Effects:*
	**Critical** – You achieved your goal, and it is even more impactful than you hoped.
	**Hit** – You succeed with a complication; you have reduced effect, you suffer a lesser injury, or you end up in a desperate situation on the enemies next action against you.
	**Miss** – You hesitate and miss your chance. You may try again by taking a risky opportunity, or withdraw and try a different approach.

###### Challenging Situation
You’re taking a chance, going head to head against something or someone that presents you with a good challenge. Some examples would be; two skilled swordsmen in a duel, 

*Effects:*
	**Critical** – Things go badly, choose two; you gain a serious injury, draw a complication, or you lose this opportunity.
	**Hit** – You succeed with a complication, choose one;  you have reduced effect, you gain a light injury, draw a complication, or your situation becomes desperate.
	**Miss** – You miss your oppourtunity. You may try again, but you will be in a desperate situation or you can try a different approach.

###### Desperate Situation
You’re reaching beyond your capabilities and attempting a difficult action you wouldn't normally try. Some examples would be; using an untrained ability, trying to fight whilst drunk, sweet talk an on duty guard to let you through.

*Effects:*
	**Critical** – You succeed with a complication, choose one; you have reduced effect, you gain a light injury, draw a complication, or your situation becomes desperate.
	**Hit** – You miss your oppourtunity. You can't try again.
	**Miss** – Things go badly. You suffer harm, a complication occurs and you lose this opportunity.

Your group have final say over the difficulty for the roll, but explain and clarify things as needed, especially when you’re starting out. 

By discussing the position (and how it might be better or worse) you’ll help everyone build a better understanding of how the fictional world operates in their minds’ eye. You’ll also set precedents that everyone can build on to make better decisions in the future. “Ah, so we got a ***advantageous*** ***Persuade*** roll when we wined and dined them and showed them how friendly we are. ##### Determine the dice and roll
The player then compares the ability score. The disparity between the two tells us how much this action can accomplish: will it have limited, standard, or great effect? The players should bare in mind whilst some things are possible, they may always be limited in scope. Not all actions are equally potent.

If a player wants to try to make a new friend by ***Forcing*** something — maybe that’s possible, but it wouldn’t be farfetched to say it’s a desperate atempt with limited effects. I would imagine it might be easier to ***Persuade*** them. 

If the ability score is higher than the difficulty, then the difficulty is simple.
If the ability score is the same as the difficulty, then the difficulty is challenging.
If the ability score is lower than the difficulty, then the difficulty is difficult.


##### Use any bonuses, or previous actions.
