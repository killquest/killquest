# The Core System
KillQuest thrives on collaboration, blending casual storytelling with structured mechanics when needed. It’s intuitive, flexible, and designed for players of all experience levels, making every session a dynamic conversation that unfolds naturally. KillQuest alternates between two types of play, ***Free Play*** and ***Structured Play***, depending on the needs of the story:

### Free Play
Free play is an open, informal conversation where players describe their characters' actions and react to the unfolding narrative. There are no turns or strict rules; players naturally take turns speaking, listening and building on each other’s ideas. This phase focuses on keeping the story flowing without interruptions from dice rolls or mechanics.

### Structured Play
When the situation becomes too complex or important, like a dangerous battle or a tense negotiation, the game shifts into structured play. Dice rolls and rules come into play to resolve challenges and ensure interesting consequences. This adds suspense and tension while keeping everyone on the same page.

### Action Phase
This begins with identifying the rewards and forming a plan or is triggered a trap and you start an encounter:
- Schemes: Players pick a target and use rolls, flashbacks and problem-solving to succeed.
- Encounters: More rigid than schemes, encounters offer fewer choices and no flashbacks, often focusing on survival.

### Engagement Roll
- Before diving into the action, this roll sets the initial conditions of the situation.

### 3. Resolution Phase
- After action scenes, downtime activities help characters recover and progress:
- Reduce stress, work on projects, or prepare for future adventures.

- Once resolved, the game flows back into free play.

### Why Use Phases?  
The phases aren’t strict rules but tools to organise gameplay. They’re like a flexible menu—choose what works best for your story. Whether you’re exploring a mysterious ruin, plotting a heist, or surviving an ambush, the phases adapt to your needs without stifling creativity.

### Action!
When entering the action state, you must work out what are the rewards available in the action and what is the plan? Action can be brought on with a Scheme or an Encounter.

This triggers the engagement roll (which establishes the situation as the operation starts) and then the game shifts into the score phase.

#### Scheme
During a scheme, the PCs decide on a target — then they make rolls, overcome obstacles, call for flashbacks, and resolve the scheme (successfully or not). When finished, the game shifts into the resolution phase.

#### Encounters
Encounters are almost identical to schemes, but offer the players no choices and don't allow for flashbacks. Often the primary goal of an encounter is just to survive, although with great risk comes great reward!

### Resolution
During the downtime phase, the GM engages the systems for rewards, heat, and entanglements, to determine all the fallout from the score. Then the PCs each get their downtime activities, such as indulging their vice to remove stress or working on a long-term project. When all the downtime activities are complete, the game returns to free play and the cycle starts over again.

The phases are a conceptual model to help you organizs the game. They’re not meant to be rigid structures that restrict your options (this is why they’re presented as amorphous blobs of ink without hard edges). Think of the phases as a menu of options to fit whatever it is you’re trying to accomplish in play. Each phase suits a different goal.
