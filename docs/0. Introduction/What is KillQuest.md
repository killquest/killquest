KillQuest is a game about assembling a group of gallant rogues to complete questionable jobs in the crumbling remains if a once mighty empire.

You should expect lethal skirmishes, faction based politics, high stakes heists and player-driven campaigns. You'll play the role of a powerful patron who oversees and controls the cast of colourful characters that are in your service. 

Within this document, you will find the essential rules to enhance your storytelling experience. Let the rules facilitate captivating narratives, inspire intrigue, and ensure the game flows smoothly. 


### How is it different to existing popular RPG’s?
- ***You don't control a single character.*** You will be playing the role of a divine patron, who controls a cult of misfits enthralled into your service. You live in the minds of your characters, a voice inside their heads they can't ignore.
- ***Democratic rulings.*** You don't need a DM, and decisions are to be agreed by a majority at the table. Although I'm sure many groups will unanimously decide to return to the old ways and install an all powerful Dungeon Master.
- ***Roleplaying, not playing roles.***  Everyone takes on the same role as a player, there is no DM, and you control multiple characters, meaning no-one has to play as a healer for the whole team. 
- ***Our rules are guidelines.*** Players should feel like they can do any action, regardless of whether it says it on the character sheet that they “can” do it. If the table agrees you can do it, you can.

##### Making it your own
This document is a distillation of best practices and useful tools that I’ve found or created. Everything in here exists to help you and your friends express yourselves by creating collaborative social fiction about a crew of daring scoundrels. You have interesting things to say, and it’s my job to inspire you and provide a framework that makes that easier. It’s not my job to tell you exactly what to do, or explain precisely how everything works.

Some elements of the game and setting are meant to emerge in play, to be discovered in an act of creative interpretation. KillQuest aims to reproduce the play style of old-school British tabletop games but in a lighter and more simple form. Everything is based around a simple and concise set of rules that you can shape as you need to fit any style of play. 

You control characters struggling to survive as they are thrust into perilous adventure by themselves and the forces of the world. The implied setting is a fantasy world in a vast Empire, beset on all sides by enemies, both within and without. Whilst we have lots of explicit setting information, you can play with your favourite published or home-made settings.

The primary focus of a director is to provide the framework that supports you and your player’s collaborative exploration. Think like a director: focusing the attention on certain moments in the narrative and using that lens to showcase the characters, set pieces, props, and conflict needed to create an impactful story. Depending on your level of experience with narrative-focused TTRPGs, this may seem like a daunting task. Use the following principles as your guide and always remember: this is a communal story game. Follow the fiction, trust your players, and always do what feels best for the story you’re all telling

- Think like a director. Frame scenes, describe shots, move the spotlight, and keep the story rolling. Set the pace and tone, and adjust based on the needs of the table. 
- Listen and incorporate. Ask the players questions about their characters and the world, then incorporate their answers into the story you’re telling.
- Only call for a roll if the consequences are interesting. If they aren’t, either it happens or it doesn’t.
- Drive the story forward. Every action a player takes and roll a player makes should have an impact on the narrative. When a player fails a roll, that shouldn’t mean the story progresses in a different direction.
---

- Make the world grounded, dangerous, and terrifying, both in a magickal and mundane way. 
- Paint the world in darkness and in light. Show the humanity in the horror and the horror in humanity. 
- Say “yes,” unless you must say “no.” Saying “yes” fosters a collaborative, creative environment for players to contribute to. Saying “no” sets boundaries on the game world that establish its rules and support its verisimilitude. You can also use “yes, but...” to reinforce the cost of players getting what they want. 
- Cut to the action. Open a scene that’s already in progress and close a scene before it loses its momentum. When players make a decision about the plan they want to take, cut to them executing that plan, rather than wading through low stakes exposition. 
- Always make a move when the fiction demands it, but especially on a mixed success or a miss. This means the moves you make don’t always need to come as the result of a roll, they can emerge organically out of the consequences of the narrative
