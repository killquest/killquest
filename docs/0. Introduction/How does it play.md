An average session is fairly similar to an episode of a TV show. There are usually one or two main events in a session, with a larger story arc that will last multiple sessions. That could take the form of complex character arcs, taking on powerful villains or strategic wars. A session will commonly last anywhere between an hour up to four hours depending on the story you are telling and the preferences of the group. 

During a session the group will usually work together to accomplish a job, wether that is supplied to them by a NPC or they create their own scheme. You will then send you characters into the action, suffer the consequences and find out if they succeed or fail. After which you'll enter free play where your characters can recover, pursue personal projects and relieve stress. From their we look for new opportunities and play again. That's a look at an average session, and the core gameplay loop.

No-one is in charge of the story, it is what happens as a result of the opening situation, the actions that every character takes, how the dice roll and how your group interprets those events. The story emerges from the convergence of all of these things.

##### How do I play?
Each character creates and controls a group of characters, that forms their crew. Characters should be interesting and fit well within the theme of your crew and the tone of your game. It is the players core responsibility to make their characters fun to play as, and with. They should seek interesting opportunities and be willing to engage with the core gameplay loop. This doesn't mean you can't play 'black sheep' characters who go against type, but don't hold up the game because of it.

##### What's it like to play?
It's a pulp action game

Achieving your goals requires cultivating a devoted following, toppling adversaries, completing quests with allies, and executing your own intricate plans. With each obstacle you overcome, you gain experience to improve your characters and expand your web of influence. 

Most of the time, the game takes the form of something between a conversation between friends and improv where the players create the world and describe the actions of their characters. 

For most of the game, you won’t be using rules, mechanics or miniatures at all. This is called unstructured play. However, when a player character makes an action that’s risky or dangerous – we roll the dice to see if they succeed or fail. Players shouldn't roll unless there’s something at stake, and someone isn't sure the character couldn't easily complete the action. We call this structured play.
